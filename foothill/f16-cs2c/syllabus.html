Detailed Course Information

Tentative Course Schedule and Content

The specific content below is tentative, but this course will cover everything in the official CS 2C course outline. There is 1 "hybrid"/online hour per week intended for homework and Canvas Discussion(s). Student participation in "hybrid" hours is mandatory.

The midterm will be on 2016-11-02 from 6:30PM to 8PM and the final will be on 2016-12-12 from 6PM to 8PM.

Week Topics Covered Relevant Chapters
1 Introduction to the world of advanced computer science. Review of vectors and templates. Introduction to time complexity. Comparative analysis of vectors and arrays. The "subset sum problem." Chapter 1, Chapter 2 "Algorithm Analysis": 2.1 (don't worry if you don't understand it)
2 In-depth analysis and implementation of vectors, linked lists, and iterators. Stacks and sparse matrices. Chapter 3 "Lists, Stacks, and Queues"
3 Complexity analysis of algorithms (big-oh, little-oh, omega, and theta). Linear, logarithmic, quadratic and other growth rates. Binary search. Proper and improper uses of recursion. Chapter 2 "Algorithm Analysis"
4 General trees. Binary search trees. Full and lazy deletion. Analysis of tree algorithms. Chapter 4 "Trees": 4.1-4.3
5 Tree balancing, AVL trees, rotation and top-down splaying. Inheritance applied to class templates. Chapter 4 "Trees": 4.4, 4.5, 4.8
6 Hash tables, hashing functions, open addressing, linear and quadratic probing, the use of prime numbers in hashing functions. Chapter 5 "Hashing" (5.6 of the 3rd edition is outdated by C++11)
7 Priority queues. Binary heaps. Complete tree implementation of binary heaps. Percolate up and percolate down. Heap sort. Chapter 6 "Priority Queues (Heaps)": 6.1-6.4
8 Insertion sort, Shellsort, Heapsort, Merge sort and time analyses of these sorting algorithms. Chapter 7 "Sorting": 7.1-7.6
9 Quick sort and indirect sorting. Review of STL maps, sets, queues and stacks. Chapter 7 "Sorting": 7.7-7.9; Chapter 3: 3.6-3.7; Chapter 4: 4.8
10 Introduction to graph theory. Vertices, edges, adjacency lists, paths and graph data structures. Shortest path algorithms and the dijkstra technique. Chapter 9 "Graph Algorithms": 9.1-9.3
11 Minimum spanning trees and the kruskal technique. The maximum flow problem. Chapter 9 "Graph Algorithms": 9.4-9.5
12 Final exam and closing remarks. None

Class Policies

Late Policy

Homeworks may be turned in almost arbitrarily late. Your score will be calculated as the average of your scores before and after the deadline.

Cheating

It's a bad way to end a relationship and it's a bad way to end a class. Please discuss all assignments/homework where the whole class can see it, for example on the Canvas forums. Any variation of copying programming lab assignments is prohibited. The assignment must be 100% your own work.

Student Learning Outcomes

C++ Inheritance

A successful student will be able to write and debug C++ programs which make use of inheritance, i.e., the "is a" relationship, common to all OOP languages. Specifically, the student will define base and derived classes and use common techniques such as method chaining in his or her programs.

Basic C++ Abstract Data Types

A successful student will be able to use the C++ environment to define the basic abstract data types (stacks, queues, lists) and iterators of those types to effectively manipulate the data in his or her program.
